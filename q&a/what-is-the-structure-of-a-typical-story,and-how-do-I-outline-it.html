What is the structure of a typical story, and how do I outline it?

Written By NT October 17, 2023
Writing-Tips

Hey, I heard you're interested in writing a short story! That's fantastic. Let me walk you through the process of structuring and outlining a story. It's not as complicated as it may seem.


I can hear that you are asking “Where should I start?” No worries, we'll take it step by step. First, let's talk about the typical structure of a story. Every good story has a beginning, middle, and end, right?


Right, but what's the structure within those parts? Good question. In the beginning, we have what's called the 'Introduction.' This is where you introduce your readers to the story's world, its characters, and the central problem or conflict. It's the part that grabs your readers' attention.


So, what's next? The 'Rising Action' comes next. Here's where you build tension and introduce challenges. It's a series of events that your characters face as the story progresses. This keeps your readers engaged. What about the most important part? That's the 'Climax.' It's the turning point in your story. The central conflict reaches its peak, and it's the most intense and pivotal moment. This is where the protagonist faces a major decision or obstacle.


So, let's say your story is about an aspiring astronaut, Sarah. In the climax, she's selected for a mission to Mars, but the spacecraft faces a critical systems failure. She has to make a life-or-death decision to save the crew. This is the 'Climax.


Wow! That sounds intense! What happens after that? After the 'Climax,' you have the 'Falling Action.' It's where the story begins to wind down. Loose ends are tied up, and the consequences of the climax become clear. It leads the story toward its conclusion.


And what's the conclusion? That's the 'Resolution.' It's where the story concludes. It provides closure to the central conflict and often offers insights or a final message. It's the last impression you leave on your readers.


This really helps break it down. Can you help me outline my story using this structure? Absolutely! Let's start with your 'Introduction' where we'll introduce your main character, set the stage, and establish the central conflict. Then, we'll move through the 'Rising Action,' 'Climax,' 'Falling Action,' and finally, the 'Resolution' to wrap it up. Remember, your story's unique characters, setting, and conflicts will make it stand out!


To summarize the thought process and a step-by-step solution: Structure of a Typical Story: Most stories follow a common structure, which includes key elements like the introduction, rising action, climax, falling action, and resolution. Here's a more detailed explanation:


Introduction (Beginning): This is where you introduce your readers to the story's world, characters, and the central conflict or problem. The introduction should engage readers and make them curious about what's to come.
Rising Action: This section is where you build the story's tension and conflict. It includes a series of events, complications, and obstacles that your characters face. The rising action keeps readers invested in the story and eager to see how it unfolds.
Climax: The climax is the story's turning point, where the main conflict reaches its peak. It's the most intense and pivotal moment of your story. It's often where the protagonist confronts their central challenge or makes a crucial decision.
Falling Action: After the climax, the story begins to wind down. Loose ends are tied up, and the consequences of the climax become clear. This section leads the story towards its conclusion.
Resolution (Ending): The resolution is where the story concludes. It provides closure to the central conflict and offers insights or a final message. It's the last impression you leave on your readers.

Creating a Story Outline: Now, let's outline a short story using this structure:


1. Introduction:

Introduce the main character, Sarah, who is an aspiring astronaut.

Set the stage by describing the futuristic world where space travel is common.

Establish the central conflict: Sarah wants to join the next mission to Mars, but she's facing stiff competition.


2. Rising Action

Sarah undergoes grueling astronaut training.

She faces challenges and develops rivalries with other trainees.

She discovers a personal connection with one of her fellow trainees, Alex.


3. Climax

Sarah and Alex are both selected for the Mars mission.

However, during the mission, they face a critical systems failure on their spacecraft.

Sarah must make a life-or-death decision to save the crew.


4. Falling Action

The crew survives thanks to Sarah's decision.

The mission continues successfully.

Sarah and Alex's relationship deepens, and they discover they share a common dream of exploring the stars.


5. Resolution

The mission to Mars is completed, and the crew returns home.

Sarah and Alex decide to pursue their dream of exploring the cosmos together.

The story ends on a hopeful note, emphasizing the power of determination and human connection.



Remember that while this is a basic outline, your story can take various forms and lengths. The key is to use this structure as a framework while allowing your creativity to shape the details of the narrative. Your story's unique characters, setting, and conflicts will make it stand out.


You've got this! Just take it step by step, and your creativity will do the rest. Happy writing!
