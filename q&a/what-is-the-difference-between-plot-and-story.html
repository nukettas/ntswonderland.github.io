What is the difference between plot and story?

Written By NT October 16, 2023
Writing-Tips

Hey, I heard you're interested in writing a short story! That's fantastic. I can hear that you are asking the difference between 'plot' and 'story' in writing. It's not as complicated as it may seem.



Think of 'plot' as the backbone of a story, the sequence of events that form the narrative. It's like a roadmap that tells you 'what happens' in the story. Okay, so that's the plot. But what about 'story'? Great question! 'Story' is a broader concept that goes beyond just the events. It's the 'how and why' of the narrative. It encompasses the characters' emotions, their personal growth, and the underlying themes or messages in the plot.

So, the story is more about the characters and their feelings? Exactly! The plot is like the external action—the battles, challenges, and encounters characters face. The story, on the other hand, takes you deeper into the characters' experiences, their internal struggles, and the lessons and themes that the plot conveys.

Let me give you an example to make it clearer. Let's take 'The Lord of the Rings' as an example. The plot describes Frodo's journey to destroy the One Ring, all the battles and challenges he faces. It's about external events and actions.

What's the 'story' part then? The 'story' in 'The Lord of the Rings' explores Frodo's inner struggles—the weight of the Ring on his psyche, his personal growth as he carries this heavy burden, and the themes of heroism, friendship, and the corrupting influence of power. It goes beyond the battles and uncovers the deeper layers of the narrative.



So, the plot is what happens, and the story is why and how it happens. Exactly! The interplay between plot and story is what makes a narrative rich and captivating. The plot provides the structure, and the story brings it to life, adding depth, meaning, and emotional resonance.


To summarize:

Plot:

The plot is the sequence of events that occur in a story. It's the "what happens" in a narrative. The plot includes the actions, events, conflicts, and resolutions that drive the story forward.

It is often linear and chronological, describing the cause-and-effect relationships that make up the story's events.

The plot is like a roadmap of the story, outlining the story's structure and progression.

Story:

The story is the broader narrative that encompasses not only the plot but also the emotional and thematic elements. It's the "how and why" of a narrative.

The story includes the characters' emotions, motivations, personal growth, and the overarching themes that provide meaning to the plot events.

It goes beyond just the sequence of events to explore the deeper layers of the narrative, often revealing the characters' inner struggles and transformation.



When explaining the difference between plot and story, it's essential to clarify that the plot is the surface-level series of events that create the story's structure and drive the narrative forward. On the other hand, the story dives deeper, exploring the characters' experiences, feelings, and the underlying messages or lessons within the plot.



Consider a popular story like "The Lord of the Rings" by J.R.R. Tolkien. The plot describes the journey of Frodo and the Fellowship to destroy the One Ring, including all the battles, challenges, and encounters they face. It's the external, action-driven part of the narrative. The story, however, delves into the emotional and thematic aspects. It explores Frodo's internal struggles, the weight of the Ring on his psyche, and the theme of heroism, friendship, and the corrupting influence of power. It goes beyond the external events and uncovers the characters' personal growth and the broader lessons of the story.



In essence, the plot provides the skeleton of the story, while the story breathes life into it, giving depth, meaning, and emotional resonance to the events. It's this interplay between plot and story that makes a narrative compelling and memorable.



If you ever have more questions about storytelling, feel free to ask. Happy writing!
